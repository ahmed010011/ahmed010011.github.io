Temple of Abu Simbel

The Great Temple of Abu Simbel, located in Nubia near egypt's southern border, is one of the most magnificent monuments in Egypt, king Ramses II of the nineteenth dynasty was completely cut in the mountain, around 1264 BC, the temple is famous for its four huge statues sitting on its façade, one of which collapsed due to an ancient earthquake and its remains are still on the ground.

The king's huge statues stand on both sides of the main hall leading to The Holy Mass, where four gods sit: Amon Ra, Ra'ah Rahtti, Petah, and Ramses II. The temple was built with high accuracy so that the sunshine in the temple enters two days a year, on February 22 and October 22, crosses the main hall, illuminates the statues deep inside the temple, and these dates are believed to correspond to the coronation and birth of Ramses II.

The two temples were relocated from their original site in 1968 after the construction of the Aswan High Dam, which threatened to flood them. The transfer was completed thanks to unesco-led international efforts. The temple was accepted on the World Heritage Site list in 1977 by the Great Property of Ramses II, Queen Nefertari. Philae Temple

Philae's monuments include many buildings dating back to the Ptolemaic period (332-30 B.C.) Most notably, the temple started by Ptolemy II Villadelvos (285-246 B.C.) Dedicated to Isis, the mother of Horus, the lord of the monarchy, there is a view in the Mamezi or the delivery room, where the birth of Horus was celebrated, showing Isis nursing her son Horus in the bushes. The Temple of Isis is one of the most persistent ancient Egyptian temples, where the temple continued to play its role until the reign of Byzantine King Justinian (527-565 AD), which ordered the closure of all pagan temples, where a priest named Asmit-Akham engraved the last Hieroglyphics text from the 4th century AD (394 AD).
